01 Introduction & Definition

Computer information systems are becoming increasingly important in daily life. Governments, private enterprises, and individuals must protect data and information against unauthorized access, disclosure, or damage. Information is now viewed as a scarce, valuable resource — and this has posed serious threats from both known and unknown sources.

Data and Information Security

Data security involves:

  • Protection of data and information against unauthorized access or modification
  • Denial of data and information to unauthorized users
  • Provision of data and information to authorized users
  • All measures taken to detect, document, and counter threats

Data and Information Privacy

Private Data

Belongs to an individual. Must not be accessed or disclosed to any other person without the owner's direct permission.

Confidential Data

Held by a government or organization about people. May be seen by many authorized persons, but must not be used for commercial gain or unofficial purposes without informing the owner.

Key Principle Both private and confidential data must be protected against unauthorized access or disclosure at all times.

02 Security Threats & Control Measures

Viruses

A computer virus is a destructive program that attaches itself to other files and installs itself without permission when the files are opened. Viruses may delete data on storage devices or interfere with proper computer functioning.

Types of Computer Viruses

Boot Sector Viruses

Destroy the booting information on storage devices.

File Viruses

Attach themselves directly to files.

Hoax Viruses

Arrive as email with an attractive subject and launch when the email is opened.

Trojans

Appear to perform useful functions but carry out undesirable activities in the background without user knowledge.

Worms

Viruses that embed and replicate themselves in computer memory.

Backdoors

May be a Trojan or worm that allows hidden, unauthorized access to a computer system.

Control Measures Against Viruses

  • Install the latest versions of anti-virus software and continuously update it with new virus definitions
  • Avoid foreign diskettes/drives in the computer room; scan them for viruses before use if necessary
  • Avoid opening mail attachments before scanning them for viruses

Unauthorized Access

Data and information is constantly threatened by people who want to access it without permission — usually with the intent to commit fraud, steal information, or destroy/corrupt data.

Forms of Unauthorized Access

Eavesdropping

Tapping into communication channels to obtain information — e.g., intercepting credit card numbers.

Surveillance / Monitoring

Keeping a profile of all computer activities of another person. Many websites track your activities using programs called cookies.

Industrial Espionage

Spying on a competitor to obtain information for sabotage or developing a similar counter-approach.

Accidental Internal Access

An employee who is not supposed to view sensitive data gets it by mistake or design.

Stranger Access

Strangers straying into the computer room when nobody is present.

Network Access

Exploitation of networked computers connected to the external world.

Control Measures Against Unauthorized Access

  • Enforce data and information access control policies on all employees
  • Encrypt data and information during transmission
  • Keep the computer room closed when not in use
  • Reinforce weak access points (doors, windows) with metallic grills and burglar alarms
  • Enforce network security measures
  • Use file passwords to deter unauthorized access to electronic files

Computer Errors and Accidental Access

Threats sometimes come from human mistakes — such as printing sensitive reports and accidentally giving them to unauthorized persons. End users with too many privileges may also cause accidental access to sensitive files.

Example A person may innocently download a file without knowing it is self-installing and dangerous to the system.

Control Measures Against Errors and Accidents

  • Assign appropriate file access privileges and roles to end users and technical staff — deny access to certain groups for certain files
  • Set up a comprehensive error recovery strategy in the organization

Theft

Some information is so valuable that competitors or governments will pay a fortune to have it stolen. Threats extend to hardware, software, and information assets.

Control Measures Against Theft

  • Employ security guards to watch over data centers and backup locations
  • Burglar-proof the computer room
  • Reinforce weak access points (windows, doors, roofing) with metallic grills and strong padlocks
  • Create backups at locations remote from the main computing centre

03 Computer Crimes

Trespass

Trespass refers to two things: (1) illegal physical entry into restricted areas where computer hardware, software, and backed-up data is kept; and (2) illegally accessing information on a local or remote computer over a network.

Hacking

A hacker is a person who intentionally breaks codes and passwords to gain unauthorized entry to computer system data and information files — violating security measures by exploiting weak access points in software.

Motivations for Hacking Some hackers enjoy the challenge; others are hired by companies to professionally expose security weaknesses. The most vulnerable targets are networked computers accessed remotely.

Tapping

A person sends an intelligent program to a host computer that relays information back to them. Another method is using special programs to spy on networked computers — intercepting messages being sent and received by unsuspecting machines.

Cracking

Cracking involves repeated guesswork until a weakness in security policies or codes is discovered. Crackers typically have partial knowledge of passwords or usernames. Another form involves finding weak access points in software.

Tip — Patches Software vendors release special corrective programs called patches to seal discovered weaknesses. Always install the latest patches for your software.

Piracy

Piracy means making illegal copies of copyrighted software, information, or data. Software and data are protected by copyright law.

Ways to Reduce Piracy

  • Enact laws that protect the owners of data and information against piracy
  • Make software affordable enough to discourage illegal copying
  • Use licenses and certificates to identify original copies
  • Set installation passwords that deter illegal software installation

Fraud

Computer fraud is the use of computers to conceal information or cheat people with the intention of gaining money or information. Fraudsters can be employees or outsiders. Some fraud involves producing and using fake documents.

Real-World Example A programmer at a tax department created a program that credited his account with tiny rounding amounts from every taxpayer. He became very wealthy before being discovered.

Sabotage

The illegal destruction of data and information with the aim of crippling service delivery or causing major losses to an organization. Usually carried out by disgruntled employees or those sent by competitors.

Alteration

The illegal changing of data and information without permission, with the aim of gaining advantage or misinforming authorized users. Usually done by those who wish to hide the truth.

Impact of Alteration Alteration of data compromises key qualities of good data: Reliability Relevance Integrity

Prevention of Alteration

  • Do not grant data editing capabilities without proper vetting
  • Require users to authenticate (sign in) before the system accepts any data alteration

04 Detection & Protection Against Computer Crimes

Having identified the dangers facing information systems, it is important to examine measures that can detect, prevent, and seal security loopholes.

Audit Trial

A careful study of an information system by experts to establish all weaknesses and vulnerable access points. An audit seeks to answer:

  • Is the information system meeting all its originally intended design objectives?
  • Have all security measures been put in place to reduce the risk of computer crimes?
  • Are the computers secured in physically restricted areas?
  • Is there adequate backup that ensures service continuity in case of a serious incident?
  • What real risks face the system at present or in the future?

Data Encryption

Data in transit over a network faces dangers of being tapped, copied, or redirected to unauthorized destinations. Data encryption protects such data by transforming it into a form that only the sender and receiver can reconstruct.

Log Files

Special system files that keep a record (log) of events on computer and resource usage. Each user is assigned a username, password, or account — allowing the system administrator to track who accessed the system, when, and what they did.

Vulnerability The most dangerous scenario is when genuine users lose or deliberately share their passwords with unauthorized users, bypassing the entire log-tracking system.

Firewalls

A firewall is a device or software system that filters data and information exchanged between different networks by enforcing the host network's access control policy. Its main aim is to monitor and control access to or from protected networks.

Inbound Protection

Remote users without permission cannot access the protected internal network.

Outbound Restriction

Users within the network cannot access firewall-restricted external sites.

05 Laws Governing Protection of Information

Although most countries are still developing their legal frameworks, the "right to privacy" is expected by all people. Countries are increasingly enacting data protection laws with provisions such as:

  • Data must not be transferred to other countries without the owner's permission
  • Data and information must be kept secure against loss or unauthorized exposure
  • Data and information must not be kept longer than necessary
  • Data and information must be accurate and up to date
  • Data and information must be collected, used, and kept for specified lawful purposes only
Call to Action Countries are encouraged to develop comprehensive data and information handling legal frameworks that protect individuals' data and information rights.
