Introduction to Networking & Data Communication

Chapter 1 — Complete Notes

Computer Network

Definition

A collection of computers linked together using transmission media/communication channels for the purpose of communication and resource sharing.

NB

A communication channel / transmission medium refers to a physical or wireless medium through which a data signal flows from source to destination.

Data Communication

Data communication refers to the process of transmitting data signals from one point to another through a network.

Communication refers to the process of using words, sounds, symbols, gestures, pictures, or signals to pass a message or information from one person to another.

Key Terms in Data Communication

Data Signal

A stream of binary data that may be represented using voltage levels in an electronic circuit.

Modulation & Demodulation

The process of converting a data signal into a form suitable for transmission over a medium. A modem modulates (converts) a digital signal into analogue form for transmission over an analogue telecommunication system. The receiving modem demodulates the incoming analogue signal back to digital form.

Multiplexing & Demultiplexing

Multiplexing is the process of sending multiple data signals over the same medium. Demultiplexing is the process of separating/splitting those signals back into multiple channels at the receiving end.

Bandwidth

The maximum amount of data that a transmission medium can carry at any one time.

Baseband Signal

A digital signal generated and applied to the transmission medium directly without modulation. The baseband signal occupies the entire transmission channel; multiple signals can be multiplexed.

Broadband Transmission

Sending an analogue signal over a transmission medium using a particular frequency. Several data signals can be sent simultaneously through the same medium at different frequencies.

Attenuation

Signal loss resulting from the decrease in strength and energy as a signal progressively moves along a transmission medium. If not regenerated (boosted), the signal may be lost before reaching its destination. Attenuation is minimised by use of repeaters.

Noise

Unwanted signals picked up by a transmission line.

Modes of Data Communication

Simplex

One direction only

  • Communication flows in only one direction
  • Example: radio transmission
Half Duplex

Both directions — one at a time

  • Communication in both directions but only one direction at a time
  • Example: police walkie-talkie
Full Duplex

Both directions simultaneously

  • Communication occurs in both directions at the same time
  • Examples: networked computers, mobile phone calls

Types of Computer Network

Local Area Network (LAN)

Formed when computers are connected in a small geographical area (e.g., floors, buildings, schools) within a radius of 10 m up to 3 km. Most LANs connect PCs and server computers. LANs are generally very fast at transmitting data.

Metropolitan Area Network (MAN)

Covers a larger area than a LAN — typically a city or town. Often used to link multiple LANs across a metropolitan region.

Wide Area Network (WAN)

Spans a very large geographical area — across cities, countries, or continents. The Internet is the largest example of a WAN.

Definitions

Server: A computer dedicated to servicing requests for resources from other computers (workstations) on the network.
Remote client: The computer making a remote request. Remote host: The computer being accessed.

Purpose of Networking

  • Resource sharing — users at one site can use resources available at another site (printers, programs, etc.).
  • Remote communication — transmission of data signals between devices at different geographical locations; people can share ideas and pass messages over the network.
  • Cost effectiveness — sharing resources reduces costs (e.g., one shared printer instead of many).
  • Distributed processing — if a particular computer is overloaded, the processing task can be distributed to other computers on the network located over a wide geographical area.
  • Reliability — if one computer breaks down, information can still be accessed from the server or other computers on the network.

Limitations of Networking

  • Cost — acquiring networking equipment, training administrators and users, and maintenance are all expensive.
  • Data security — networked data is more prone to illegal access, theft, and tapping by unauthorised individuals during transmission.
  • Network failure — failure can paralyse operations of an organisation and damage files and programs.
  • Moral and cultural effects — large networks like the Internet can expose users to harmful or inappropriate content.

Data Communication Media

Data communication media is the channel through which data is transmitted between computers and other devices. Media is categorised into two types:

Bounded (Wired) Media

Unbounded (Wireless) Media

Two-Wire Open Line Cables

Made up of two parallel copper wires separated by a plastic insulator. Used in telecommunication networks to transmit voice signals. Easily affected by noise from radio frequencies, resulting in poor transmission.

Twisted Pair Cable

Consists of pairs of insulated copper wires twisted in a spiral pattern to minimise electromagnetic interference.

Types

UTP — Unshielded Twisted Pair
  • No shield against electromagnetic interference
  • Susceptible to noise and signal interference
  • Cheaper and easier to install
STP — Shielded Twisted Pair
  • Has a braid wrapped around the wires
  • Protected from noise and interference
  • Slightly more expensive

Twisted pair cables are categorised into six groups (Cat 1–6) according to the type of data transmitted and maximum rate of transmission.

Advantages
  • Easy to install
  • Very cheap (cable and equipment)
Disadvantages
  • Low data transmission rates
  • Sensitive to electromagnetic interference
  • High attenuation — requires repeaters for long distances (every ~90 m)

Coaxial Cable

Has a central copper core (solid or stranded) surrounded by an insulator, which is then surrounded by a hollow mesh conductor covered by a shield to prevent electromagnetic interference. Bandwidths up to 1 Gbps; used to form network backbones.

Types

  • Thinnet — thinner, more flexible, easier to install.
  • Thicknet — thicker, two insulators around the core, used for longer distances.
Advantages
  • Immune to external electrical interference
  • High data transmission rate (up to 1 Gbps)
  • Can carry more signals
  • Stable under high loads
  • Carries voice, data, and video simultaneously
Disadvantages
  • Lack of security due to illegal taps
  • Relatively expensive
  • Bulky and difficult to install
  • LANs using coaxial are difficult to troubleshoot and maintain

Fibre Optic Cables

Made of glass fibres that transmit information in the form of light. Composed of:

  • Core — central transparent plastic or glass part
  • Cladding — protective layer surrounding the core with light-bending (refraction) characteristics
  • Buffer — surrounds the cladding to strengthen the cable
  • Jacket — outer covering

Types of Fibre Optic Cables

Single Mode Fibre
  • Very narrow centre core
  • Light takes only one path — low attenuation
  • Preferred for long-distance transmission
  • Bandwidth: up to 50 Gbps
Multimode Fibre
  • Thicker core; multiple light rays fed in at an angle
  • Higher attenuation — used for shorter distances
  • Light undergoes refraction along the cable

How Fibre-Based Networks Transmit Data

Data from the source is converted to a light signal. An LED (Light Emitting Diode) converts the data into a light signal. A photodetector at the other end converts the light signal back to a data signal.

Advantages
  • High bandwidth and carrying capacity — very fast
  • Immune to electromagnetic interference
  • Covers large distances (low attenuation)
  • Very secure — cannot be tapped
  • Safe in hazardous/inflammable areas (no electrical signal)
  • Small and lighter than copper cables
Disadvantages
  • Connectivity devices and media are very expensive
  • Installation is difficult
  • Requires specialist knowledge

Wireless Communication (Unbounded Media)

Transmits data from one point to another without physical connections — through space using electromagnetic waves.

Electromagnetic Spectrum

All electromagnetic waves travel at the speed of light. For networking: radio waves, microwaves, and infrared waves are most relevant.

Microwave Transmission

Signals travel in a straight line — transmitter and receiver must be in direct line of sight of each other (point-to-point transmission).

Advantages
  • Operates at high speed
  • Less prone to transmission errors than twisted wire pairs
  • Capable of operating with digital or analogue data
Disadvantages
  • Signal affected by atmospheric conditions (lightning, etc.)
  • Requires additional repeaters every few kilometres

Satellite Transmission

Microwave radio signals are transmitted from parabolic dish antennae on the ground to a satellite in orbit. The signal is received at an uplink frequency, amplified, and re-broadcast on a different downlink frequency to many earth stations (point-to-multipoint).

Components of a Satellite Transmission System

  • Transmitter earth station — sets up the uplink to the satellite.
  • Geostationary satellite — orbits ~36,000 km from earth; rotates at the same speed as the earth so it appears stationary to an observer on the ground. It receives, amplifies, and retransmits the signal.
  • Receiver earth station — receives the downlinked signal.

VSAT Technology

Very Small Aperture Terminal (VSAT) — a very small satellite dish used for data, radio, and TV communication. Enables direct access to satellite communication without going through the state-owned satellite gateway.

Advantages
  • Cost is not dependent on distance
  • Second highest bandwidth after fibre optic
  • Earth stations can be installed on customer property
Disadvantages
  • Expensive to install ground stations
  • Heavy rain or bad weather increases signal loss

Radio Communication & Bluetooth

Radio Waves

Radio waves are omnidirectional — they spread outwards in all directions from a central point. Radiated into the atmosphere by a radio frequency antenna. Three frequencies are used:

  • HF (High Frequency) — directed to the ionosphere; reflected back to earth's surface.
  • VHF (Very High Frequency) — transmitted along the earth's surface.
  • UHF (Ultra-High Frequency) — used for point-to-point communication.

Bluetooth Technology

A short-range radio transmission technology enabling handheld communication devices (mobile phones, PDAs) to access the Internet and communicate wirelessly within a limited area. A network of Bluetooth-enabled devices is called a Wireless Personal Area Network (WPAN) or piconet.

Infrared Transmission

Communication is achieved via infrared transmitters and receivers (transceivers). The transmitter and receiver must be within line of sight in the same room. Unlike radio signals, infrared signals cannot penetrate walls or other obstacles.

Advantages & Disadvantages of All Wireless Media

Advantages
  • Can cover larger geographical areas easily
  • Flexible — devices can be moved without losing network access
  • Satellite communication can reach remote areas with no physical infrastructure
Disadvantages
  • Initial cost is very high
  • Relatively difficult to establish or configure

Communication Devices

Modem

Modulator-Demodulator

  • Connects a computer to a telephone line
  • Converts digital ↔ analogue signal
Hub / Concentrator

Basic Signal Broadcaster

  • Connects computers on a network
  • Broadcasts messages to ALL nodes — each node decides which to receive
Switch

Intelligent Hub

  • Forwards packets directly to the addressed node (no broadcasting)
  • Point-to-point / packet switching
Repeater

Signal Booster

  • Receives a signal from one network segment
  • Cleans, boosts, and retransmits it to the next segment
Router

Network Interconnector

  • Interconnects different networks
  • Directs data packets from source to destination
Bridge

Selective Forwarder

  • Determines the appropriate network segment for delivery via address filtering
Gateway

WAN / Internet Access

  • Any device configured to provide access to a WAN or the Internet (e.g., a router or computer)
NIC

Network Interface Card

  • Circuit board fitted in an expansion slot or on-board on the motherboard
  • Creates a connection between the computer and the transmission media

Wireless Communication Devices

Access Points (AP)

Provide wireless access to portable devices (PDAs, laptops). Devices need antennas to detect signals in the surrounding area.

PCMCIA Cards

Personal Computer Memory Card International Association (PCMCIA) — an add-on card inserted into a PDA or laptop to enable wireless communication between the device and a wired network server.

Network Software

Programs that manage a network, enforcing security, interoperability, load balancing, and resource sharing. Includes:

  • Network Operating Systems
  • Protocols

Network Operating System (NOS)

Functions of a NOS include:

  • Making sharing of resources possible
  • Transmitting data around the network
  • Responding to requests from application programs
  • Enabling nodes to communicate efficiently
  • Implementing network security features
  • Supporting services

Protocols & the OSI Model

Protocol

A set of rules and procedures that govern communication between different computers on a network.

Protocols are developed using guidelines provided by the Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) reference model. Data transmission is broken into discrete systematic layers — each layer has its own rules and procedures. Co-ordination is achieved through protocol layering.

Common Protocols

  • TCP/IP (Transmission Control Protocol / Internet Protocol) — TCP ensures delivery of sequenced data over the network; IP handles the addressing scheme, packet forwarding, and routing. The main internetworking protocol enabling the Internet.
  • FTP (File Transfer Protocol) — allows users to connect to remote systems and send/receive files.
  • OSPF (Open Shortest Path First) — used by routers to forward packets through the shortest and least-loaded path.
  • NetBEUI — a local area network protocol (limited to LAN environments).

Network Topologies

The way in which computers and other devices are arranged, or how data passes from one computer to another in the network.

Logical Topology

The way data passes from one device to the next on the network.

  • Ethernet topology — all computers listen to the network; only one can send at a time.
  • Token ring topology — a token (data packet) circulates the network; only the computer whose address is on the token takes it, reads the data, then releases it.

Physical Topology

The physical layout or arrangement of components on the network.

Star Topology

All devices are connected to a central hub or switch. Data passes from a transmitting computer through the hub/switch to its destination. A hub broadcasts to all nodes; a switch forwards directly to the addressed node.

Advantages
  • Easy to configure
  • Easily expanded
  • One workstation failing does not affect others
  • Allows centralisation of key resources (e.g., server)
Disadvantages
  • If the central hub/switch fails, the entire network goes down
  • Requires more cables — more expensive
  • Time-consuming to install

Ring Topology

All components are connected to a single cable forming a ring. Data flows in only one direction; each node actively retransmits the signal to its neighbour if the message is not addressed to it.

Advantages
  • Simple to install
  • Very fast data transmission
  • Uses short cable length — cheap
Disadvantages
  • One failure affects all nodes
  • Troubleshooting is difficult
  • Adding or removing a device can disrupt the entire network

Mesh Topology

Uses separate cables to connect each device to every other device on the network, providing a direct communication path between any two nodes.

Advantages
  • Easy to troubleshoot
  • Network can still operate when a node or cable fails
  • Very reliable
  • Point-to-point connection optimises throughput
Disadvantages
  • Costly to provide redundant links
  • Difficult to install and maintain
  • Network administration is complex due to multiple connections

Tree / Hierarchical Topology

All nodes are connected in the form of a tree — nodes as leaves, links as branches. Branches are connected via hubs. Can be viewed as a collection of star networks arranged hierarchically (star topologies connected via a bus).

Advantage
  • If a workstation below the main bus fails, the network continues to function
Disadvantages
  • If the backbone fails, the entire network fails
  • Installation is expensive and complex

Bus Topology

All devices are connected to a central cable called the bus or backbone. All nodes receive transmission, but only one node can transmit at any one time. Terminators are attached on both ends of the cable to prevent signal bounce-back.

Advantages
  • Easy to install
  • Less expensive — no complete cable length per node
  • One node failing does not affect others
Disadvantages
  • Failure of the main bus/backbone brings the whole network down
  • Only one node can transmit at a time
  • Difficult to troubleshoot

Hybrid Topology

A combination of any two basic network topologies. Most WANs are configured as hybrid topologies. A tree topology is considered hybrid because it combines hierarchical star networks connected to a root node.

Network Security

Share-Level Security

Mostly used in peer-to-peer networks. The user decides which resources to share. Most Windows operating systems provide this type of security.

User-Level Security

Used on server-based networks. A network administrator assigns accounts to users — each user has a unique username and password to access network resources.

Practical: Setting Up a Peer-to-Peer LAN

Hardware Requirements

  • Computers running Microsoft Windows (9x/2000/ME/XP or later)
  • Network Interface Cards (NIC) — some motherboards have on-board NICs
  • A hub or switch
  • UTP Category 5 cable
  • RJ45 connectors
  • Crimping tool and cable tester

Installing a Network Card (Add-on)

  1. Disconnect the computer from the power source.
  2. Open the system unit and identify an empty expansion slot on the motherboard.
  3. Insert the card and screw it into place. Avoid touching the golden conductor points or chip with bare hands.
  4. Replace the casing, reconnect power, and boot up. Windows plug-and-play should detect the card automatically.

Adding Protocols

  1. Open the Network dialog box.
  2. Click Add → Protocols.
  3. Select Microsoft, then choose TCP/IP and NetBEUI from the list → OK.
  4. Click Apply.

Configuring TCP/IP

  1. Select the TCP/IP Ethernet adapter from the installed components list.
  2. Click Properties → Specify an IP address (e.g., 192.168.0.1). Each computer must have a unique IP address.
  3. Specify the subnet mask (e.g., 255.255.255.0) — allows the router to distinguish the network ID from the host ID.
  4. Click Apply → OK and restart the computer.

Attaching an RJ45 Connector to a UTP Cable

  1. Cut a piece of cable (~3 metres).
  2. Strip approximately 2 cm of sheath on both ends to expose the inner pairs.
  3. Untwist the pairs and arrange the wires in the correct order using the wiring diagram.
  4. Insert the wires into the RJ45 connector in correct order and push into the crimping tool.
  5. Squeeze the handles firmly to pierce and hold the wires.
  6. Repeat for the other end. Test the cable with a cable tester for continuity and correct termination.

Connecting Computers to the Hub/Switch

  1. Connect one end of the terminated cable to the NIC's RJ45 port and the other to a port on the hub/switch. An LED on the hub indicates a live connection.
  2. Repeat for all computers to be networked.
  3. Right-click Network Neighbourhood / My Network Places on the desktop to verify successful networking.

Testing the Connection (PING)

  1. From the Start menu of the computer with IP 192.168.0.1, choose Run and type: PING 192.168.0.2
  2. Click OK. A successful reply confirms network connectivity; "Request Timed Out" indicates a failure.

Sharing Network Resources (Windows XP)

  1. Open Windows Explorer.
  2. Right-click the drive, folder, or file you want to share → Sharing and Security.
  3. Click the Sharing tab → Enable "Share this folder".
  4. Type a share name and set other options as required.

Review Questions & Answers

Define the term computer network.
A collection of computers connected together using transmission media for the purpose of communication and resource sharing.
Differentiate between baseband and broadband signals.
Baseband refers to a digital signal generated and applied to the transmission medium directly without modulation. Broadband transmission refers to sending an analogue signal over a transmission medium at a particular frequency, allowing multiple signals to be sent simultaneously at different frequencies.
Distinguish between a network server and a workstation.
A network server is a computer dedicated to servicing requests for resources from other computers on the network. A workstation is a computer connected to a network on which users do their work.
Why is a switch preferred to a hub on a network?
Unlike a hub, a switch forwards packets directly to the addressed node without broadcasting to all nodes, making communication more efficient and secure.
Differentiate between single mode and multimode fibre optic cables.
Single mode fibre has a very narrow centre core, allowing light to take only one path, resulting in low attenuation — preferred for long-distance transmission. Multimode fibre has a thicker core that allows multiple light rays to enter at different angles, resulting in higher attenuation — mainly used for shorter distances.
Describe VSAT technology.
VSAT (Very Small Aperture Terminal) refers to a very small satellite dish used for data, radio, and TV communication. It enables direct access to satellite communication instead of having to go through the state-owned satellite gateway.
Explain the concept of a geostationary satellite.
A geostationary satellite orbits the earth at approximately 36,000 km altitude at the same rotational speed as the earth. To an observer on earth, the satellite appears stationary in the sky, maintaining a fixed focus on earth stations.
Compare and contrast a repeater and a router.
A repeater receives a weak signal on the network, cleans (removes distortion), amplifies, and retransmits it to the next segment of the same network. A router interconnects different (separate) networks and intelligently directs data packets from source to destination using routing protocols.
Distinguish between logical and physical network topology.
Logical topology (also called signal topology) deals with the way data passes from one device to the next on the network. Physical topology refers to the actual physical layout or arrangement of components on the network.
List the seven OSI model layers.
1. Physical   2. Data Link   3. Network   4. Transport   5. Session   6. Presentation   7. Application
What type of network is characterised by over 254 computers connected to share resources in a city?
A Metropolitan Area Network (MAN).
Differentiate between microwave and radio transmission.
Microwave transmission is point-to-point — the transmitter and receiver must be in direct line of sight. Radio transmission is omnidirectional — signals originate from a central point and spread outward in all directions.
