01 — Employment

Effects on Employment

The introduction of computers in the workplace has resulted in three major employment shifts: the creation of new jobs, the replacement of computer-illiterate workers, and the displacement of formerly manual jobs.

Job Creation

ICT has introduced employment opportunities that never existed before. The use of computers in financial institutions, reservation systems, educational institutions, and communications has created entirely new roles.

  • Computer operators & programmers
  • Network administrators & IT/IS managers
  • Database administrators & software developers
  • System analysts

Job Replacement

Certain jobs disappear in one form but reappear requiring higher-skilled manpower. Many clerical and repetitive tasks have been made redundant, and computer-illiterate employees have been replaced by those with the required digital skills.

Job Displacement

Employees are moved to departments where computer skills are not required — typically those unwilling to acquire new skills. To retain competent staff, most employers organise regular in-service training to help employees keep up with rapid ICT changes.

02 — Production

Effects on Automated Production

Automation refers to substituting mechanical and electronic devices for manual ones to improve quality and speed. Manufacturing industries such as vehicle assembly plants, oil refineries, and food processing companies use computers to automate processes, with computer-controlled robots and assembly lines now commonplace.

✓ Advantages
  • Increased efficiency from balanced workload and production capacity
  • Improved customer service through high-quality goods
  • Faster delivery and timely production
  • Efficient utilisation of resources — less operating expense
  • Reduced accidents in hazardous sectors (mining, chemicals)
✗ Disadvantages
  • High initial cost of setup
  • Can cause unemployment in labour-intensive areas
  • Possible hardware/software incompatibility
  • Over-reliance leads to laziness
  • Resistance to change among employees

Why Employees Resist Automated Systems

  • Fear of change — people are creatures of habit
  • Fear of job loss — computers are associated with redundancies
  • Fear of failure — anxiety about never adapting to new systems
  • Loss of control — management fears losing organisational oversight
  • Lack of understanding — not seeing the benefits leads to treating computers as intruders
03 — Health

Issues of Workers' Health

🤲
Repetitive Strain Injuries (RSI)
Injuries from wrist, hand, arm, and muscle strain due to forced repetitive movement — e.g. continuous keyboard data entry. Remedies: rest, relaxed posture, changed typing techniques.
👁
Eye Strain & Headaches
Prolonged close-range monitor use causes Computer Vision Syndrome (CVS): eye strain, headaches, double vision, fatigue. Use good-resolution monitors with antiglare screens and take regular breaks.
⚡
Electromagnetic Emissions
Waves of electrical and magnetic energy emitted by current-carrying conductors. Users are advised to use low-emission devices to reduce exposure.
🧠
Mental Stress
Workers feel pressure to match computer speed and productivity. Those monitored by computers experience additional performance anxiety.
🚶
Isolation
People conduct business and communicate without face-to-face contact. Operators can work entire shifts without personal conversation, leading to feelings of social isolation.
☢️
Radiation & VDU / Chip Toxins
VDUs have been linked to birth defects, cancer, and miscarriages. Workers in chip manufacturing are exposed to toxic chemicals posing similar risks.
Ergonomics — the science of designing the workplace for worker comfort and safety. Better working environments have driven the design of special furniture to prevent backaches and specially designed monitors to reduce eye strain.
04 — Environment

Environmental Issues

Energy Consumption & Radiation

Early computers consumed vast amounts of energy, generating heat and emitting harmful electromagnetic radiation. Modern devices must now be Energy Star compliant — a standard launched by the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) to encourage minimal power use.

Electronic Waste

Huge dumps of dead computer parts, printers, ink toner cartridges, monitors and accessories are disposed in landfills, causing significant environmental pollution. Of particular concern are Nickel-cadmium laptop batteries, which contain toxic cadmium that can leak into underground water tables and catchment areas when buried.

05 — Culture & Crime

Cultural Effects & Computer Crimes

Moral Effects

The rapid growth of ICT challenges moral and cultural values. It has changed how we communicate and has affected privacy, human rights, and integrity. Issues include exposure to flaming (derogatory online messages), access to pornographic material, and the use of computers to forge documents. On a positive note, ICT is used as a campaign platform against diseases like AIDS and drug abuse.

Computer Crimes

The use of computer software for illegal purposes. Computer-related crimes — hacking, eavesdropping, and piracy — are on the increase.

Crime Type Description
Data ManipulationAltering data entering or exiting a computer, e.g. changing data on a certificate.
Time BombCoding a program to destroy itself after running a set number of times.
Computer VirusA program designed to destroy or alter data and spread to networked computers.
Trap DoorCreating a special password giving its creator sole system access.
Data StealingUsing a computer to steal information gathered for someone else.
EavesdroppingTapping unauthorised communication lines carrying computer data.
Industrial EspionageStealing trade secrets (designs, marketing plans) from one company and selling them to another.
PiracyMaking unauthorised copies of copyrighted software.
FraudUsing computers to cheat people for financial gain or information.
SabotageIllegally destroying data and information to cause organisational loss.
Logic BombA program executed when a specific condition is met, e.g. a fired employee's delayed attack on records.
06 — Breakthroughs

Breakthroughs in ICT

Significant breakthroughs have been achieved across multiple sectors:

  • Health Care
  • Education
  • Communication
  • Research
  • Commerce
  • Art & Design
  • Entertainment
  • Transport

Communication breakthroughs include fax, e-mail, and the internet. Commerce has been transformed through digital shipping and tracking of imports and exports. Entertainment now encompasses digital music, video, and games.

07 — Evolution

Evolution of Computer Systems

Future trends in ICT will be characterised by three major themes:

  1. Rapid evolution in computer hardware and software
  2. Artificial intelligence
  3. Expanded information superhighway

Rapid Hardware & Software Evolution

The silicon revolution has seen rapid increases in microprocessor speeds and capabilities. Future trends will see a reduction in size and cost of components alongside increased capability. Microprocessors the size of a thumb can now execute instructions in a trillionth of a second, enabling smaller but smarter devices. Computers will become more intelligent — able to learn from their environment, understand human voice, and respond accordingly. Software will be more versatile and easier to use.

08 — Artificial Intelligence

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

A branch of computer science concerned with developing machines that emulate human-like qualities such as learning, reasoning, communicating, seeing, and hearing. AI developed from the need to create programs that could challenge humans in games like chess and scrabble.

7 Main Application Areas

Expert Systems

Software that makes a computer operate at a human expert's level in a narrow specialisation.

Natural Language Processing

Enabling computers to recognise and understand natural languages, spoken or written.

Voice Recognition

Systems that allow voice input via microphone, with challenges around homophones and accents.

Voice Synthesis

Machines that create human-like speech, e.g. a computerised bank teller reading your balance.

Computer Vision

Processing and interpreting light waves to read and interpret text and objects visually.

Neural Networks

Electronic systems that emulate the neurological structure of the human brain.

Robotics

Computer-controlled devices that emulate humans for dangerous or difficult tasks.

Expert Systems — In Detail

Expert systems simulate the reasoning of specialists in areas such as medical diagnosis, financial forecasting, locomotive maintenance, and geological exploration.

Components

  • Knowledge Base — stores relevant facts, beliefs, assumptions, and problem-solving procedures.
  • Inference Engine — software that controls the knowledge search and produces conclusions.
  • User Interface — the display screen enabling user interaction.

✓ Advantages of Expert Systems
  • Perform tasks faster than humans
  • Easily identify equipment faults
  • Store far more information than a human
  • Impartial and consistent recommendations
  • Never forget or make memory errors
  • Data kept up to date
  • Available 24 hours, will never retire
  • Usable remotely over a network
✗ Disadvantages of Expert Systems
  • Can make mistakes and not learn from them
  • Work best for specific, well-defined problems — complex and costly to design
  • Lack human touch — cannot be questioned further
  • Heavy use can displace human experts

Robotics

✓ Advantages of Robots
  • Work 24/7 without breaks or fatigue
  • Faster than human workers
  • Very high and consistent productivity
  • Consistently high output quality
  • High degrees of accuracy
  • Can operate in hostile or dangerous conditions
✗ Disadvantages of Robots
  • Expensive to build, install and maintain
  • Inflexible — designed for a single job
  • Malfunction can cause chaos or faulty goods
  • Displace human workers, causing unemployment
  • Risk of misuse in warfare or biohazard manipulation

The Three Laws of Robotics

  1. A robot must not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.
  2. A robot must always obey orders given by a human being, except where doing so would conflict with the First Law.
  3. A robot must protect its own existence, except where doing so would conflict with the First or Second Law.

Artificial Neural Networks

The use of electronic devices and software to emulate the neurological structure of the human brain. Artificial neurodes perceive environmental stimuli and decide whether to pass signals on — mirroring biological neurons. Key attributes include pattern recognition in large data sets and the ability to make predictions and identify anomalies.

Banking Example: Credit card spending patterns can be tracked over time. If the card is lost or stolen, the bank can detect unusual spending patterns and take security measures even before the loss is officially reported.
09 — Superhighway

Expanded Information Superhighway

Involves the integration of cabled and wireless technologies for data and information transmission. Key developments include:

  • Vast increase in throughput of transmission media such as fibre optic and wireless technologies.
  • Scientists have demonstrated a fibre optic cable — the diameter of a single strand of hair — capable of carrying a trillion bits per second.
  • The Internet is growing tremendously, leading to what is called a digital explosion or digital hurricane of the information superhighway.
